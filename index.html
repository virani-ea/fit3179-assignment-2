Victorian Healthcare Access Snapshot

Are hospitals located where people live? How do socioeconomic factors influence healthcare equity?

This visualisation explores healthcare access across Victoria, starting with a statewide overview and then zooming in
to highlight regional equity and disparities. The interactive maps and charts reveal how hospital locations,
population, and socioeconomic factors shape access. Designed for policymakers and the public.

Statewide Overview

Population Density and Hospital Locations

It is evident that the majority of hospitals are positioned
in or near high-density regions, supporting health service
accessibility in populous urban zones.

However, the map also reveals large, low-density areas with few
or no hospitals, raising questions about healthcare access for
rural communities.

These spatial insights prompt further investigation into service
availability and equity outside major cities.

How does Victoria compare to other states?

This chart compares Australian states and territories by the number
of public hospitals per 100,000 residents—a basic indicator of
potential healthcare access.

While densely populated regions like NSW and Victoria have the
highest absolute numbers of hospitals, smaller states (i.e.
Tasmania and South Australia) have more hospitals per
capita, suggesting strong access.

ACT stands out for its uniquely low ratio, largely due to its
city-state structure. This perspective highlights regional
inequalities in infrastructure, which can have implications for
healthcare delivery and resilience.

Ambulance Response Time by Decile

How to Read This Chart

The following plot displays ambulance response times for
Victorian LGAs by SEIFA decile. Each box shows the range,
spread, and median of response times within a decile.
Comparing boxes across deciles highlights differences
between disadvantaged and advantaged areas.

This chart explores how socioeconomic disadvantage correlates
with ambulance response times across Victoria.

A clear pattern emerges — response times tend to be longer
in more disadvantaged regions, with greater variability as well.
This suggests that residents in lower-decile LGAs may face slower
emergency response due to factors such as lower service density,
resource constraints, or distance from urban centers.

By contrast, wealthier and more urban LGAs (higher deciles)
generally report faster and more consistent response times,
highlighting disparities in emergency care accessibility. These
findings reinforce the broader theme of unequal healthcare
access and underscore the importance of equitable resource
distribution across Victoria’s regions.

Hospital Accessibility Across SEIFA Deciles

How to Read This Chart

This scatter plot summarises hospital density (hospitals/square
kilometre) for Victorian LGAs grouped by SEIFA decile. Each
point summarises hospital density within a specific
socioeconomic decile. The y-axis represents relative advantage
(higher decile = more advantage), and the x-axis shows
hospital density. Comparing these helps identify patterns or
differences in hospital accessibility across socioeconomic groups.

How Strongly Does Disadvantage Affect Hospital Accessibility?
The plot shows that higher SEIFA decile areas do not
always correspond to greater hospital density; there are
several affluent LGAs with low density.

However, as decile increases, there is more variation in
hospital density: decile 2 LGAs consistently have very low hospital
density (below 0.05), while deciles 7 and above show some LGAs
with substantially higher densities.

Thus, though the relationship between geographic advantage and
hospital access may be inconsistent, geographic factors remain an important influence.
